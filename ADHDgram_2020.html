People-ing is hard (reprise)

Dear friends,

I was talking to Liz over at Healthy ADHD last week about balancing our unique needs and sensitivities with our obligations and responsibilities to others.

Others look to us to do or be certain things. Sometimes their expectations are unreasonable, but sometimes perfectly reasonable expectations conflict with our brain chemistry.

This comes out in a lot of places in our lives, but the holidays lay it on thick. Here are some common triggers:

  • Big family gatherings feel overstimulating
  • We get overwhelmed by the demands of hosting, traveling, and gift shopping
  • Less time at work leaves us playing catch-up
  • Kids stay up late, get off their regular routine, and perhaps get overwhelmed and overstimulated as easily as we do
  • Extended family members may self-medicate their own unmanaged ADHD by arguing with us about politics, drinking too much, criticizing our life choices, etc.
  • Family may look to us to organize gift shopping, travel schedules, packing, etc. while they go about business as usual
  • Parents or in-laws compare us to our siblings or their children, question our decision to medicate our children's ADHD, and/or give unsolicited parenting advice during a stressful moment
  • We stay up late and make unhealthy choices with food and alcohol, which leaves us feeling yucky the next day
  • Visiting family (or family we’re visiting) eats and sleeps on a different schedule than we do and expects us to go with the flow
  • We forget our meds

I could go on! People with ADHD can’t always go with the flow. Disruptions and triggers often have a bigger effect on us than they would a neurotypical person.

That’s life. If you’re an extroverted, go-with-the-flow type person, great! If you look forward to the holidays with a mix of excitement, exhaustion, joy, and resentment, my tip of the month below is for you

In Order From Chaos I talk a lot about the person we see in ourselves. Who do you want to be? What role do you want to play in your family? What are your deepest values?

During the holidays we often struggle to be that person for our families without driving ourselves crazy.

My tip this month is: take care of yourself so you can be there for others.

I’m an introvert who married into a large family of enthusiastic socializers. While I tried to roll with them for the first few years we knew each other, age and parenthood eventually caught up to me. I don’t have the energy I used to have. I can’t stay up until the wee hours of the morning, nor can I spend every waking hour involved in conversation. 

And yet it’s tough to do my own thing when we stay with people who have a very different lifestyle. Here are the coping strategies I’ve developed for those multi-day visits:

  • Give hosts a heads up about our schedule. When we stay with family or friends I always mention what time we eat dinner and go to bed. Both my kid and I are a hot mess if we don’t eat dinner around 6:00. He goes to bed at 7:30 or 8:00. A 7:30 dinner reservation simply won't cut it. Most hosts prefer to know this and accommodate it rather than have you be miserable the whole time you’re with them.
  • Take small chunks of time for myself: go for a run around the neighborhood, read a book in another room, excuse myself to catch up on some work.
  • Go to bed on time. Yes, that means I miss out on social time. Many adults stay up later than I do. I like to be asleep by 11:00 and up by 6:30. I wake up by 7:00 regardless, so any late-night shenanigans translate directly to lost sleep.
  • Take my meds. No explanation needed here, this is not the time to forget meds.
  • Put my kiddo to bed (roughly) on time. The only thing worse than being underslept and overstimulated myself is having my kiddo feel the same way. If I let him stay up late for a special event or party, I make sure he’s in bed on time both nights before and after.
  • Don’t say yes to everything. I say no to a board game if I think I may be asleep in my chair by the time it’s over. If I’m not interested in the movie everyone’s watching in the family room, I go read a book instead — movies aren’t quality time anyway. This gives me more energy for the stuff I really shouldn't flake out on.
  • Be willing to look uncool. Every one of the above sometimes makes me look different than the rest of the crowd. Some make me seem less fun or cool. All increase my ability to actually be fun and cool during the time I spend with friends and family.

I hope this helps some of you as well. It’s a jungle out there!
